How to stop the generational sinking ship of mental health's decline

Andrew turned 21 in January. His party was celebrated with family and friends in his family home, showered in chocolates, celebratory drinks and happy faces posing for group photos. The night was filled with drunken laughter and happy wishes.

Andrew’s funeral was on the 3rd of July, that same happy crowd saying their goodbyes. This is the tragic situation amongst many New Zealanders of today, with it only becoming more frequent; no voice, no help, nowhere to turn. For someone who had quietly suffered for years, he was failed by the system that promised to help him.

There were over 20 Andrews for every 100,000 young adults last year.

One thing is made clear by these statistics: New Zealand needs change. Among the countless news articles and voices speaking out, three main questions need to be addressed.

How bad is it?

Have we made any progress?

What’s in store for our youth to come?

The present

Let’s recap what we know already.

2nd worst in the developed world for youth suicide rates.

Top 25% on nearly every measure of depression of 18 different countries across the world.

Statistics show this trend is worsening, with suspected suicide cases in 2023/2024 remaining consistent with last year’s, according to the October 2024 Chief Coroner’s report.

The Finance Service Council’s findings from April 2024 confirm what many believe: mental health and wellbeing are now the country’s top health concerns.

Kerry Gibson, a lecturer at The University of Auckland, has closely studied youth mental health worldwide. She regularly encounters New Zealand’s mental healthcare system, which she describes as “overwhelmed” and “outdated.”

“Every now and again, somebody will phone and ask me for help on how to negotiate the system. And a lot of the time I'm stumped.”

Gibson is a clinical psychologist specializing in youth mental health, along with being known as the former President of the New Zealand Psychological Society.

The Past

The 1990s saw important advances in the mental health services offered around New Zealand, though the biggest changes date back to 1973. Deinstitutionalization began in the country following a demand for outpatient care, with the mentally ill becoming more embraced by the wider public.

Institutionalization saw hundreds of people across the world being locked into institutional facilities with horrific quality of care. 1846 was when “Lunatic Ordinance” had anyone deemed a “lunatic” by two doctors and a magistrate put into prisons or hospitals. Though when those got overcrowded, asylums were built.

Johnny Matteson, a musician and mental health worker from New Zealand, needed support at a time when Aotearoa New Zealand didn’t openly accept mental illness. Instead of getting the help he needed, “It all just happened so quickly”, and he was admitted to Carrington Psychiatric Hospital.

“I was there for six months – it was extremely traumatic and changed the rest of my life.”

Mental Health Foundation of NZ has shared Johnny’s story from his time as a 19-year-old in misplaced ‘healthcare’.

Gibson, despite studying current-day mental health, acknowledges that “our systems are very much based on people who think in a very linear kind of way,”

Maddy Kear has over 26 years of experience as a counselor, with 6 in the UK and the other 20 in New Zealand. She has worked with clients of all ages, along with the Salvation Army Bridge Program. Recently, she’s seen a significant increase in people seeking her guidance. Her extensive experience has made her a trusted support for those facing life’s challenges across generations.

She comments on the changes she’s seen over her years working in the “underpaid and overworked” Aotearoa New Zealand mental health industry.

“It’s concerning. There are more and more people coming forward with less and less support.”

“Only the very imminent cases get seen, the others tend to take a backseat in the priority list.”

Counselor, Maddy Kear

The Future

Kudrat Mast, High School Student

Kudrat Mast celebrates her final year of high school this year. She is Head Girl of her school, with public speeches and final exams on her agenda to conclude her secondary education. She has been accepted into Auckland University on a scholarship and regularly achieves excellent grades, yet also encounters the struggles that come hand in hand with mental well-being.

Gibson also comments on educational stresses on youth.

“Young people are certainly reporting that they feel much more pressured at school, that there are many more demands on them and, and demands to succeed.”

High School has statistically proven itself to be a mentally challenging moment for many New Zealand youth, with a quarter of students experiencing very high levels of psychological distress.

Mast confirms this, describing her high school end of year exam atmosphere as “strange” and “daunting”.

“It feels so uncertain knowing whether what I have studied or how I have studied will pay off, and I just worry that it won't be enough.”

A September 2024 Youthline survey saw 55% of 15–24-year-olds in New Zealand agreeing with Kudrat, saying that stress was their biggest issue within their lives- particularly exams, study and homework.

“There is so much left to do,”

As excited as she is for university next year, she’s equally daunted for the big new step.

“We also have an amazing school counselor who I also have a good relationship with, as I have seen her variously throughout my schooling career. All the staff are supportive and lend an ear, shoulder and hand whenever we need one.” -Kudrat

This generation has, according to the auditor-general of New Zealand, seen the mental health of younger kiwis decline ‘rapidly over the past decade’.

Kia Manawanui Aotearoa is the government’s 10-year initiative plan for transforming New Zealand’s mental wellbeing. This has been separated into 3 groups.

Building the foundations (2021–2023)
Expanding innovation (2023–2027)
Embedding system-level change (2027–2031)

So, as many people ask, what now? What will change for New Zealand’s children? More may be on the horizon than meets the eye.

One piece of conclusive evidence is the barriers of mental health being broken down with each passing generation. From politicians publicly announcing their battles with their own mental health struggles, to embracing their neurodivergent identities.

Technology has proven itself to be far more influential than ever seen possible, with advancements progressing even in the mental health sector. Gibson comments on the dangers of social media, but also highlights that technology “also offers a space to connect for lots of people, it can be quite a creative space”. She also points out that there is no conclusive result for whether social media causes more mental distress or helps people to stay connected to each other and access mental health support.

The Ministry's first published Suicide Prevention Action Plan in 2019 is on the Ministry of Health's website, setting a 10-year strategy divided into 5-year intervals. This mental health plan focuses on long-term goals, specific outcomes, key priorities, and shared responsibility to create a brighter future. With the current 5-year phase ending soon, the Ministry has opened a public consultation to update the plan, which will close on November 1.
